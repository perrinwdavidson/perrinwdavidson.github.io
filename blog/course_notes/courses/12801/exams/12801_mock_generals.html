(a) The leading order balances in the large-scale dynamics of the ocean are those that dictate the gyre circulation, as is shown in the map. To get the forms of the equations that govern this motion, we start with deriving the Navier-Stokes equation, from which we will make assumptions using scaling rules to get to the main equations dictating our flow. We start with Newton's Second Law, applied to a water parcel of finite volume $V = \delta x \delta y \delta z$ for $\delta_i$ the dimensions in the $x, y, z$ coordinate directions, respectively: \begin{equation} \Sigma \underline{F} = m \underline{a} \sim \: [\text{N}], \end{equation} where we defined $\Sigma \underline{F}$ as the sum of all forces acting on the parcel. However, we note that our interest lies in taking the volume of this water parcel to 0, i.e. infinitesimal. To do this, we will divide through by $V$ to get: \begin{equation} \Sigma \underline{\mathcal{F}} = \rho \underline{a} \sim \: [\text{N m}^{-3}], \end{equation} where we have noted that $\rho = m / V$. Now, we start with the 3 leading forces in incompressible fluid motion: \begin{equation} \Sigma \underline{\mathcal{F}} = \mathcal{F}_{p} + \mathcal{F}_g + \mathcal{F}_v, \end{equation} and we go through each of these now:

  1. Pressure Gradient Force: $\mathcal{F}_p = -\nabla p$.
  2. Gravitational Force: $\mathcal{F}_g = -g\rho \hat{k} = - \rho \nabla \Phi$ for $g$ the acceleration in vertical $\hat{k}$ direction and $\Phi$ the geopotential.
  3. Viscous Force: $\mathcal{F}_v = \mu \Delta \underline{u}$, for $\underline{u}$ the fluid velocity, $\Delta$ the Laplacian operator, and $\mu$ the dynamic viscosity.

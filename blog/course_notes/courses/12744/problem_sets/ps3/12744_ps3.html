12.744: Problem Set 3

Problem 1

Calculations

We first want to assume a model in which all anthropogenic forcings are placed into the atmosphere, which we define as follows: \begin{equation} \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}t}\left[\left(C_a \circ \delta^{13}C_{a}\right)(t)\right] = F(t)\delta^{13}C_a, \end{equation} where $\left(C_a \circ \delta^{13}C_{a}\right)(t)$ is the weighted product of total carbon in a resevoir denoted by the subscript and the isotope for the same resevoir. Here, we define $F$ as the emission forcing given by: \begin{equation} F(t) = \left(\frac{2(C_T - \Delta t)}{\Delta t} + r_0\right)\tilde{t} + r_0, \end{equation} which provides: \begin{equation} \widehat{F}(t) = \int\text{d}F = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{2(C_T - \Delta t)}{\Delta t} + r_0\right)\tilde{t}^2 + r_0\tilde{t}, \end{equation} for $C_T$ the total emissions over the period $\Delta t$ (i.e., 1950 to 2020), $r_0$ the initial rate of emission (1 [Gt y$^{-1}$]), and $\tilde{t} = t - 1950$. This is simply the primitive of the linear increase described in the problem, modified to make the rate self-contained with the total emissions discussed in the problem. Specifically, we change $C_T$, which we do in the top panel (solid and dashed lines) of Fig. 1, to induce different emission scenarios. The solid line scenario is for $C_T = 440$ [Gt y$^{-1}$] and the dashed line is for $C_T = 881 - 612 = 269$ [Gt y$^{-1}$].

Now, we can define the mixing relationship used to calculate the isotope values by integrating the first equation, using Separation of Variables to get: \begin{equation} \int_{t'\in\left[t_0,t\right]} \text{d}\left[\left(C_a \circ \delta^{13}C_{a}\right)(t')\right] = \int_{t_0}^t \text{d}t' \text{F}(t')\delta^{13}C_a, \end{equation} which gives: \begin{equation} C_a(t)\delta^{13}C_a(t) = C_a(t_0)\delta^{13}C_a(t_0) + \widehat{F}(t)\delta^{13}C_{p}, \end{equation} where $\delta^{13}C_p$ is the constant isotope value for anthropogenic emissions and we assume $t_0=1950$ is at pre-industrial levels, making $C_a(t_0)$ the pre-industrial value of carbon in the atmosphere. We present the solutions for the two different emission scenarios in the middle plot of Fig. 1 (solid and dashed).

Next, we define a piece-wise forcing function for $\Delta^{14}C$ of the form: \begin{equation} \Delta^{14}C_s(t) = \begin{cases} -20 & 1950 \le t < 1955 \\ -20 + 75 \tilde{t} & 1955 \le t < 1965 \\ 730 & 1965 \le t \end{cases} \end{equation} This is based on analysis and discussion from Graven et al. (2017). We then correct for decay by: \begin{equation} \Delta^{14}C(t) = \Delta^{14}C_s(t)\exp\left[\lambda\left(t_p - \widehat{t}\right)\right], \end{equation} where $t_p$ is the data of production (starting in 1955 and ending in 1965), $\widehat{t} = t - 1955$, and $\lambda = \ln 2 / 5730$ [y$^{-1}$]. We plot $\Delta^{14}C(t)$ in the bottom plot of Fig. 1.

Comparison to Data

From the problem, we plot the piece-wise "observed" data (other than the actual observational data, in the dotted line in the middle panel of Fig. 1) in the dot-dash line in the middle plot of Fig. 1, for which we use the following equation: \begin{equation} \delta^{13}C(t) = -6.6 -0.025\tilde{t}, \end{equation} from the problem. We can clearly see that there is a large discrepency propogated through time between the expected $\delta^{13}C$ values in both emission scenario and the observation record. This is due to the absence of either both the ocean and the biosphere (solid line) and just the biosphere (dashed line). This so called "Suess Effect" is greatly exaggerated in these scenarios given the lack of these resevoirs.

Similar to $\delta^{13}C$, we can see from the bottom panel of Fig. 1, that $\Delta^{14}C$ is nearly a translated Heaviside function given the rapid rate of bomb testing for a decade in the mid 20th century. The long decay constant all makes it so that the decay has a negligible effect on the reduction of the isotope in the atmosphere. Compared to the data, the dotted line in the bottom panel of Fig. 1, we see that the lack of either resevoir sink has a much larger effect than $\delta^{13}C$, with a change nearly two orders of magnitude greater ($\mathcal{O}(10^0)$ vs. $\mathcal{O}(10^2)$), leading to the quasi-exponential change in the atmospheric resevoir.

Problem 2

Center of Mass

We define the "center of mass" equation to define the mean of multiple variables in this problem set. We define the center of mass for a variable $x$ with corresponding "mass" (or measurement) $m$ as: \begin{equation} \overline{x} = \int \text{d}x \: x\cdot m(x) \bigg/ \int \text{d}x \: m(x). \end{equation}

Mean Age

Applying this formula to the emissions defined by $\widehat{F}$, we get that: \begin{equation} \overline{\tau} = \int \text{d}t' \: \tilde{t}\cdot \widehat{F}(t') \bigg/ \int \text{d}t' \: \widehat{F}(t') \simeq 51 \:\left[\text{y}\right]. \end{equation} which is approximately 2001. We note that we could also find the time at which 50$\%$ of total emissions had been emitted using the quadratic formula for a polynomial of the form: \begin{equation} \left(\frac{2(C_T - \Delta t)}{\Delta t} + r_0\right)\tilde{t}^2 + r_0\tilde{t} - (440 / 2) = 0, \end{equation} for which the positive part of the quadratic formula gives $\overline{\tau} \simeq 48$ [y] (or approximately 1998), but for sake of consistency with later calculations, we use the center of mass calculation. This line is plotted as the vertical dot-dashed red line in the top panel of Fig. 1 and Fig. 3.

Mean Penetration Depth

We now apply the center of mass calculation, which is graphically represented in the inset plot of Fig. 2, to determine the mean penetration depth of the radiocarbon. We get, for $\Delta\delta^{13}C(z)$ the difference between the two observed profiles: \begin{equation} \overline{Z} = \int \text{d}z' \: z'\cdot \Delta\delta^{13}C(z') \bigg/ \int \text{d}z' \: \Delta\delta^{13}C(z') \simeq -231 \:\left[\text{m}\right]. \end{equation} We also note that this value is of course integrated over the time since the bomb testing ceased (in our model we assume that this is in 1965). Therefore, we can define a rate: \begin{equation} r_{\overline{Z}} = \frac{\overline{Z}}{1986 - 1965} \simeq -11 \: \left[\text{m}\right]. \end{equation}

Mean Penetration Depth over $\overline{\tau}$

Next, we can apply this rate to find the penetration depth over the mean age of the bomb carbon: \begin{equation} \langle \overline{Z} \rangle = r_{\overline{Z}} \overline{\tau} \simeq -564 \: \left[\text{m}\right]. \end{equation}

Problem 3

First, we can calculate the amount of DIC, which we call $C_d$, that can be exchange with the atmosphere. To do this, we calculate the percentage of the total ocean that is covered by the penetration depth, as follows: \begin{equation} r_{p} = \frac{\text{SA}}{\text{V}}\langle\overline{Z}\rangle \simeq 0.16, \end{equation} where we have used values of SA $= 360 \times 10^{12}$ $[\text{m}^{2}]$ and V $= 1.29 \times 10 ^{18}$ [m$^{3}$] from Sarmiento and Gruber (2006). However, we note that this assumes that the DIC is homogeneous in the ocean. As given in the problem, we know that this is not the case, but rather that the $\delta^{13}C(z)$ decreases with a given power law relationship to 0 [‰]. Backed by observational data (e.g., Schmitter and Lund 2015, Fig. 4) we know that conversely, $C_d(z)$, and here we assume therefore the amount of available DIC to exchange with the atmosphere, increases asymptotically with depth, which we assume is of the form: \begin{equation} f_d(z) = 1 - \exp\left[-z / L_z\right], \end{equation} where we define $L_z$ as the $e$-folding depth. We note that this is simply a fraction relating what we define as the available DIC from a non-homgeneous assumption to that of the homogeneous assumption. After tests with observational data (the green marker in Fig. 4), we conclude that $L_z = 1000$ [m]. Therefore, we conclude that amount of aviable DIC is given by: \begin{equation} C_d = r_pf_d(\langle\overline{Z}\rangle)\text{DIC}_T \simeq 2.579 \times 10^{3} \:\left[\text{Gt C}\right], \end{equation} where DIC$_T$ is the total resevoir of DIC in the ocean (38,000 [Gt C]).

Now, we imagine a model in which there is some flux of anthropogenic emissions into the ocean, which can be described by the set of equations: \begin{align} \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}t}\left[\left(C_a \circ \delta^{13}C_{a}\right)(t)\right] &= \left(F(t) - G(t)\right)\delta^{13}C_a, \\ \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}t}\left[\left(C_o \circ \delta^{13}C_{o}\right)(t)\right] &= G(t)\delta^{13}C_p, \end{align} where $G(t)$ is a function representing the transfer of anthropogenic carbon into the ocean, which is unknown. Integrating the equation for the ocean with respect to time gives: \begin{equation} \delta^{13}C_o(t) = \frac{C_d}{C_d + G(t)\Delta t}\delta^{13}C_d + \frac{G(t)\Delta t}{C_d + G(t)\Delta t}\delta^{13}C_p, \end{equation} where we note that $G(t)\Delta t$ is the total amount (or change) of carbon that goes into the ocean from the atmosphere. We then integrate the equation for the atmosphere similarly and solve for $G(t)\Delta t$ to find: \begin{equation} G(t)\Delta t = \left( C_a(t)\delta^{13}C_a(t) - C_a(t_0)\delta^{13}C_a(t_0) - \widehat{F}(t | C_T = 269)\delta^{13}C_p \right) \frac{1}{\delta^{13}C_p}, \end{equation} where we have corrected the primitive of the emissions rate to be scenario of interest here (the atmosphere and the ocean are the resevoirs, of which the ocean takes all carbon the atmosphere is observed to not to given the emissions scenario). As we are interested in the change of $\delta^{13}C_d$, which we denote $\Delta_{t_0}\delta^{13}C$ where the $\Delta_{t_0}$ operator means the difference in the value at time $t$ and $t_0$, we can rearrange this equation to get: \begin{equation} \Delta_{t_0}\delta^{13}C_d = \left(\Delta_{t_0}\delta^{13}C_o - \frac{G(t)\Delta t}{C_d + G(t)\Delta t}\delta^{13}C_p \right) \frac{C_d + G(t)\Delta t}{C_d}, \end{equation} where we define $\Delta_{t_0}\delta^{13}C_o$ as the difference between the dashed line and the dash-dot line in the middle panel of Fig 1, that is the difference between the $\delta^{13}C_o$ when all emissions not stored in the atmosphere go into the ocean and the observed value in the atmosphere. The result is plotted in Fig 3. in the solid line ove time, compared to the observed value from the problem in the dashed line. The inset is the difference between the two plots over time, and the dot-dashed line is the mean age as calculated in Problem 2.

Now, we are interested in how this compares with the expected change in $\delta^{13}C$ with the observed surface marine DIC value (the dashed line in Fig. 3) over $\overline{\tau}$. We can see from the inset in Fig. 3 that the difference between the calculated and observed values is $\sim 2.1$ [‰]. Specifically, we can compare these by calculating the rate over $\overline{\tau}$, i.e. $r_{\Delta\delta^{13}C_d} = \Delta_{t_0}\delta^{13}C_d(\overline{\tau}) / \overline{\tau}$: \begin{align} \text{Expected/Calculated} &: -5.3 \times 10^{-2} \: \left[‰ \: y^{-1}\right]\\ \text{Observed} &: -1.2 \times 10^{-2} \: \left[‰ \: y^{-1}\right] \end{align} Clearly, there is a discrepency between the observation and the mass balance in the model that we have been considering up to this point. We see that there is more $\delta^{13}C$ in the ocean system than observed by a factor of $\sim 4 \text{ to }5.$ That is large!

Problem 4 and 5

We now address this contradiction between observation and mass balance. To do this, we first imagine an ocean box model in which some portion of the anthropogenic carbon is partitioned into the biosphere, leading to a model of the form: \begin{equation} \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}t}\left[\left(C_o \circ \delta^{13}C_{o}\right)(t)\right] = \left(G(t) - H(t)\right)\delta^{13}C_p, \end{equation} where $H(t)$ is said additional function we are now considering. We can solve this as we have previosly and solve for $H(t)\Delta t$ to get to an equation of the form: \begin{equation} H(t)\Delta t = \left(C_d\Delta_{t_0}\delta^{13}C_d + G(t)\Delta t \Delta_{t_0}\delta^{13}C_p - \left( C_d + G(t)\Delta t\right)\Delta\delta^{13}C_o\right) \frac{1}{\delta^{13}C_p - \Delta_{t_0}\delta^{13}C_o}, \end{equation} where we have updated $\Delta_{t_0}\delta^{13}C_d$ from the solution in Problem 3 (the solid line in Fig. 3) to the observed (the dashed line in Fig. 3). We plot the solution to this equation in Fig. 4 in the blue solid line.

Lastly, to calculate the change in the biospheric $\delta^{13}C$, we consider a model in which there is a function input of anthropogenic carbon into the biosphere: \begin{equation} \frac{\textrm{d}}{\textrm{d}t}\left[\left(C_b \circ \delta^{13}C_{b}\right)(t)\right] = H(t)\delta^{13}C_p, \end{equation} which, integrating this model with respect to time as before and taking the operator $\Delta_{t_0}$, gives: \begin{equation} \Delta_{t_0}\delta^{13}C_b(t) = \frac{C_b}{C_b + H(t)\Delta t}\delta^{13}C_b + \frac{H(t)\Delta t}{C_d + H(t)\Delta t}\delta^{13}C_p - \delta^{13}C_b(t_0) \end{equation} We plot this solution as the solid red line in Fig. 4.

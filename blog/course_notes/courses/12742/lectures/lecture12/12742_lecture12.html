Thermodynamics in Seawater

Gibbs Free Energy

For chemical reactions, we can define: \begin{equation} \Delta G = \sum G_{prof} - \sum G_{react} = \sum_i v_i \mu_i, \end{equation} where $v_i$ is the stoichiometric coefficient and $\mu_i$ the chemical potential. This is the change in free energy or the chemical work that is being done in the system.

An Example

As an example, we can take: \begin{equation} \text{HCO}_3^- \leftrightarrows \text{H}^+ + \text{CO}_3^{2-}, \end{equation} then we have that: \begin{equation} \Delta G^0 = \mu_\text{H} + \mu_{\text{CO}_3^{2-}} + \mu_{\text{HCO}_3^-}. \end{equation} The factors that contribute to our potential are:
  1. concentrations
  2. chemical nature ($G^0, \Delta G^0, \mu^0$)
  3. interactions between species, such as non-ideal (like seawater)
We can define: \begin{equation} \mu_i = \mu_i^0 + \underbrace{RT \log \left[i\right]}_{\text{concentration}} = \mu_i^0 + RT\log a_i, \end{equation} where we define the activity as: \begin{equation} a_i = \gamma_i\left[i\right] = \{i\}, \end{equation} for $\gamma_i$ the activity coefficient. \begin{equation} \Delta G = \mu_\text{H} + \mu_{\text{CO}_3^{2-}} + \mu_{\text{HCO}_3^-} + RT\log\left[\frac{a_\text{H}a_{\text{CO}_3^{2-}}}{a_{\text{HCO}_3^{-}}}\right] = \Delta G^0 + RT \log Q, \end{equation} where we note that $Q$ is the ratio of reactants over products. Now, at equilibrium, we know that $\Delta G = 0$ (i.e., we are at a steady state). Therefore, we can rearrange to get: \begin{equation} Q = \exp\left[- \Delta G^0 / RT\right] = K_{eq}, \end{equation} which, at our equilibrium, is our reaction coefficient. Now, to tackle the activity coefficients, we note that: \begin{equation} \mu_i = \mu_i^0 + RT\left( \log\gamma_i + \log\left[i\right]\right). \end{equation} These are super significant as they drive reactions in seawater, as there are myriad reactions going on. In seawater, the major driving force is the ionic strength of seawater. Typically, we can relate: \begin{equation} \log\gamma_i \propto \sqrt{I}, \end{equation} for $I$ the ionic strength defined as: \begin{equation} I = \frac{1}{2} \sum_i \left[i\right]z_i^2, \end{equation} for $z_i$ the charge of $i$. As some examples, we know:
  1. 1.0 M NaCl: $I = 1$
  2. 1.0 M MgCl$_2$: $I = 3$
  3. SW: $I \sim 0.7$
There are few things that we now recall:
  1. Debye-Huckel: $\log\gamma_i= -A z_i^2\sqrt{I}$, for $I <0.01$ M
  2. Davies (ion-ion): $\log\gamma_\pm = -Az_+z_-\left( \frac{\sqrt{I}}{1 + \sqrt{I}} \right) - bI$, for $I < 0.5$ M
  3. Pitzer: super complicated, but the gold standard for $I <$ saturation
Now, we are going to define the thermodynamic equilibrium constant: \begin{equation} K_{thermo} = \frac{a_\text{H}a_{\text{CO}_3^{2-}}}{a_{\text{HCO}_3^{-}}} = \frac{\gamma_{\text{H}^+}\left[\text{H}^+ \right] \gamma_{\text{CO}_3^{2-}}\left[\text{CO}_3^{2-} \right] }{\gamma_{\text{HCO}_3^{-}}\left[\text{HCO}_3^- \right]}. \end{equation} Then, we can define the concentration equilibrium constant as: \begin{equation} K_{conc} = \frac{\left[\text{H}^+\right]\left[\text{CO}_3^{2-}\right]}{\left[\text{HCO}_3^{-}\right]}, \end{equation} which allows us to define: \begin{equation} K_{thermo} = \frac{\gamma_{\text{H}^+}\gamma_{\text{CO}_3^{2-}}}{\gamma_{\text{HCO}_3^{-}}} K_{conc}. \end{equation} Now, the issue here is that electrodes, which is what we use to measure things in the lab, measure the activity not the concentration of H$^{+}$ ions. To combat this, we define the "mixed acidity constant" for freshwater as: \begin{equation} K' = \frac{a_{\text{H}^+}\left[\text{CO}_{3}^{2-}\right]}{\left[\text{HCO}_3^{-}\right]} = K_{thermo}\frac{\gamma_{\text{CO}_3^{2-}}}{\gamma_{\text{HCO}_3^-}}. \end{equation} What we note is that we have only treated these are individual and not as species that are able to form ion-ion pairs. There are a few kinds of bonds:
  1. Direct ion pair: coulombic ion pair, such as for Na$^{+}$ and Cl$^{-}$
  2. Hydration sphere contact: localized hydrolysis, which is a water separated ion pair
  3. Shared hydration sphere: water-like sharing

Ion Pairing

Magnesium Ion

Let's take an ion: \begin{equation} \left[\text{Mg}^{2+}\right]_T = \left[\text{Mg}^{2+}\right] + \left[\text{MgOH}^+\right] + \left[\text{MgCO}_2\right] + \cdots \end{equation} This then means that $\left[\text{Mg}^{2+}\right] \not= \left[\text{Mg}^{2+}\right]$. As an example: \begin{equation} \left[\text{Mg}^{2+}\right] + \left[\text{SO}_4^{2-}\right] \to \text{MgSO}_4. \end{equation} Then, we can say that: \begin{equation} K_{\text{MgSO}_4} = \frac{\{\text{MgSO}_4\} }{\{\text{Mg}^{2+}\}\{\text{SO}_4^{2-}\}} \simeq 12.9. \end{equation} Therefore, apply our definition of the equilibrium constant from above, we get that: \begin{equation} \{\text{MgSO}_4\} \simeq 12.9 \{\text{Mg}^{2+}\}\{\text{SO}_4^{2-}\}. \end{equation} In order to get these values from the 1960s, there was a large minimization problem following:
  1. Assume (wrongly) that $\left[\text{Mg}^{2+}\right]_T = \left[\text{Mg}^{2+}\right]$, the free species
  2. Then, to correct this, form $\left[\text{MgSO}_4\right]$ and add this back into $\left[\text{Mg}^{2+}\right]_T$, which was not larger than the observed value
  3. Next, adjust the free ion until
  4. Convergence was reached for all species
The free species activities are what drive much of the chemistry within the ocean. Additionally, we see that Magnesium itself, given that the free species is at 87% abundance in the ocean, is a major catalyst for ionic interactions in the ocean. Importantly, over time, we note that these are not at steady state and very much change over time, such as between the LGM and now.

Carbonate Ion

We can start by defining: \begin{equation} \{\text{CO}_3^{2-}\} = \gamma_{\text{CO}_3^{2-}}^T\left[\text{CO}_3^{2-}\right]_T, \end{equation} from which we can get: \begin{equation} \gamma_{\text{CO}_3^{2-}}^T = \gamma_{\text{CO}_3^{2-}}\frac{\left[\text{CO}_3^{2-}\right]}{\left[\text{CO}_3^{2-}\right]_T} \simeq 0.02. \end{equation}

Comparing to Seawater

We again define for freshwater: \begin{equation} K_{a_2}' = \frac{\{\text{H}^+\}\left[\text{CO}_3^{2-}\right]}{\left[\text{HCO}_3^{-}\right]} = K_{thermo} \frac{\gamma_{\text{CO}_3^{2-}}}{\gamma_{\text{HCO}_3^-}}. \end{equation} Now, we set for seawater: \begin{equation} K_{a_2}^{app} = K_{a_2}^\ast = \frac{\{\text{H}^+\}\left[\text{CO}_3^{2-}\right]_T}{\left[\text{CO}_3^-\right]_T} = K_{a_2}' \frac{\gamma^T_{\text{CO}_3^{2-}}}{\gamma^T_{\text{HCO}_3^-}} \simeq 10^{-8.88}. \end{equation} Contrast this with: $K_{a_2}' \simeq 10^{-10.33}.$ That's more than an order of magnitude difference! We can then compare: \begin{equation} \frac{\left[\text{CO}_3^{2-}\right]_T}{\left[\text{HCO}_3^-\right]_T}, \end{equation} which shows us that at pH $=8$, we have that freshwater is better at buffering than seawater as in seawater we are already more saturated in carbonate ion than freshwater.

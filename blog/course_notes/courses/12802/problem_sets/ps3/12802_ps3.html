Problem 1

(a) We want to derive an approximation for the potential vorticity of a 1-layer homogenous model on the $\beta$-plane with a constant depth $H$ in the absence of external forcing (i.e. it is conserved): \begin{equation} \frac{\textrm{D}}{\textrm{D}t}\left[\frac{f + \zeta}{h}\right] = 0, \end{equation} where we define the following terms: \begin{align} \zeta &= \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}, \\ f &= f_0 + \beta y, \\ h &= H + \eta, \end{align} and the derivative is the material derivative, defined as $\partial_t + \underline{u}\cdot\nabla$. To derive this approximation, we first note and suppose a few things: \begin{align} |\eta| &\ll |H|, \\ |\zeta| &\ll |f_0|, \\ |\beta y| &\ll |f_0|. \end{align} We further remember the following useful Taylor expansion: \begin{equation} \frac{1}{1 - x} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x^n. \end{equation} So, we first can see: \begin{equation} \frac{f + \zeta}{h} = \frac{\zeta + f}{H}\frac{1}{\left(1 + \eta / H\right)}. \end{equation} Then, we can apply the Taylor expansion from above to the second fraction and only consider first order terms, which gives: \begin{equation} \frac{\zeta + f}{H}\left(1 - \frac{\eta}{H}\right) = \frac{1}{H}\left(\zeta + f - \zeta \frac{\eta}{H} - f_0\frac{\eta}{H} - \beta y \frac{\eta}{H}\right). \end{equation} By the suppositions above, we can reduce this to: \begin{equation} \frac{1}{H}\left(\zeta + f - f_0 \frac{\eta}{H}\right). \end{equation} As $1 / H$ and $f_0$ are constants, by plugging in the above equation to the material derivative, we can simplify this expression to: \begin{equation} \frac{\textrm{D}}{\textrm{D}t}\left[\zeta + \beta y - f_0 \frac{\eta}{H}\right] = 0. \end{equation}

(b) We now want to linearize the equation from above about a rest state, i.e. $\overline{\underline{u}} = \overline{\eta} = 0$ via perturbation quantities of order $\epsilon \ll 1$, i.e.: \begin{align} u\left(x, y, t\right) &= \epsilon u'\left(x, y, t\right), \\ v\left(x, y, t\right) &= \epsilon v'\left(x, y, t\right), \\ \eta\left(x, y, t\right) &= \epsilon \eta'\left(x, y, t\right), \end{align} where we let: \begin{align} u' &= - \frac{f_0}{g} \partial_y \eta, \\ v' &= \frac{f_0}{g} \partial_x \eta. \end{align} Plugging these perturbation quantities into the equation of interest, we get the following: \begin{align} \frac{\textrm{D}}{\textrm{D}t}\left[\zeta + \beta y - f_0 \frac{\eta}{H}\right] = \partial_t\left[\epsilon\partial_xv' - \epsilon\partial_yu' - \epsilon\frac{f_0}{H}\eta' \right] + \epsilon \beta v'\partial_yy =0, \end{align} where we have neglected all terms higher than $\epsilon$, and noting that $\partial_{x_i} x_j = \delta_{ij}$ (for $x_i,x_j$ coordinates $x,y$), we get the linearized equation: \begin{equation} \partial_{tx}v' - \partial_{ty}u' + \beta v' - \frac{f_0}{H} \partial_t \eta' = 0. \end{equation} Now, we can plug in our geostrophic equations for $u', v'$ giving: \begin{equation} \frac{g}{f_0} \partial_{tx x}\eta + \frac{g}{f_0}\partial_{t y y} \eta' + \frac{g}{f_0}\beta\partial_x\eta' - \frac{f_0}{H}\partial_t \eta' = 0. \end{equation} Noting that this is an equation only of sea surface anamoly $\eta'$, we assume a solution of the form $\eta' \sim \eta_0 \exp\left[i\left(kx+ly-\omega t\right)\right]$. Plugging this into our linear equation of $\eta'$ and solving for $\omega$ we get the dispersion relation: \begin{equation} \omega = - \frac{\beta k}{K^2 + k_d^2}, \end{equation} where $K^2 = k^2 + l^2$ is the horizontal wave number and $k_d^2 = \left(f_0^2 / gH\right)$ is the Rossby Deformation Radius. We note that this is the dispersion relation for Rossby waves with a finite deformation radius linearized around a rest state (i.e., $\overline{\underline{u}}=0.$)

Problem 2

(a) We remember the governing equations for Poincaré waves: \begin{align} \partial_t u' - f_0v' &= -g \partial_x \eta', \\ \partial_t v' + f_0u' &= -g \partial_y \eta', \\ \partial_t \eta' + H\left(\partial_x u' + \partial_y v' \right) &= 0, \end{align} which is a linarization of the rotating, flat-bottomed $f$-plane shallow water equations about a state of rest. We then note that we can derive PDEs just of $u$ and $v$ alone by taking the time derivative, consequently eliminating the opposite variable gives equations, and again taking $\eta \sim \eta_0 \exp\left[i\left(kx+ly-\omega t\right)\right]$: \begin{align} \partial_{tt}v + f_0^2v &= -gf\partial_x\eta - g\partial_{ty}\eta = \left(-gl\omega - \text{i}gf_0k\right) \eta_0\exp\left[i\left(kx+ly-\omega t\right)\right], \\ \partial_{tt}u + f_0^2u &= -gf\partial_x\eta - g\partial_{ty}\eta = \left(-gk \omega - \text{i}gf_0l\right) \eta_0\exp\left[i\left(kx+ly-\omega t\right)\right]. \end{align} Now, for $l = 0$, we note that the terms above, from the mass conservation equation, the ansatz for $\eta$ (which together give a term of the form $\omega \sim kH$), and taking the non-zero parts (imaginary for $v$ or real for $u$) of the velocity equations, we get: \begin{align} u &= \frac{\omega \eta_0}{kH}\cos\left[kx-\omega t\right] \sim -\frac{\omega}{kH}\cos\left[kx\right]\cos\left[\omega t\right], \\ v &= \frac{f_0 \eta_0}{kH}\sin\left[kx-\omega t\right] \sim -\frac{f_0}{kH}\sin\left[kx\right]\cos\left[\omega t\right], \end{align} where the last identity is derived from assuming a comination of Poincaré waves of the form: \begin{align} \sin(\cos)\left[kx + \omega t\right] + \sin(\cos)\left[kx - \omega t\right] &= 2\sin(\cos)\left[kx\right]\cos(\cos)\left[\omega t\right], \\ \end{align} Now, we note that Gill (1982) also considers the solution with initial conditions given by: \begin{equation} \eta = -\eta_0 \text{sgn}\left[x\right], \end{equation} which, for the equation: \begin{equation} \partial_{tt} \eta - c^2\left(\partial_{x x}\eta + \partial_{y y}\eta\right) + f^2\eta = 0, \end{equation} derived from the linearized governing equations above, we get solutions of the form, again for $l=0$: \begin{align} u &= \frac{2\omega\eta_0}{kH\pi}\int_0^\infty \text{d}k \frac{k\cos\left[kx\right]\sin\left[\omega t\right]}{k^2 + a^{-2}}, \\ v &= \frac{2f_0\eta_0}{kH\pi}\int_0^\infty \text{d}k \frac{k\cos\left[kx\right]\cos\left[\omega t\right]}{k^2 + a^{-2}}, \end{align} for $c^2 = gH$. In particular, $u$ has solutions of the form: \begin{equation} u = \begin{cases} (g \eta_0 / c)J_0\left(f_0\left(t^2 - x^2 / c^2\right)^{1 / 2}\right) & |x| < ct, \\ 0 & |x| > ct, \end{cases} \end{equation} for $J_0$ a Bessel function of order zero and $a = (gH)^{1 / 2} / |f|$.

(b) We now discuss the implications of Poincaré waves on linearized PV (i.e., $\zeta - f_0 \eta' / H$). We start by noting that the wave dispersion for these waves can be found by first assuming that: \begin{equation} \eta' \sim \eta_0\exp\left[\text{i}\left(kx+ly - \omega t\right)\right]. \end{equation} Plugging this into our linearized rotating, flat-bottomed $f$-plane shallow water equations about a state of rest, we get: \begin{equation} \omega^2 = f_0^2 + c^2K^2. \end{equation} The Poincaré waves are therefore governed by the linearized PV defined by, as in the $f$-plane as discussed above: $\zeta - f_0 \eta' / H$. This then means that these waves do not affect linearized PV, given that they are derived from the same linearization as that performed for the PV. We can further see this in the dispersion relation, as we get a linear equation that is a summation of the $f$-plane and the affect of vorticity $\zeta$, which comes from $K^2$.

Problem 3

(a) We will again linearize our conservation of potential vorticity equation, this time, however, we assume a uniform zonal flow, $\overline{u}$. This leads to the perturbation quantities: \begin{align} u\left(x, y, t\right) &= \overline{u} + \epsilon u'\left(x, y, t\right), \\ v\left(x, y, t\right) &= \epsilon v'\left(x, y, t\right), \\ \eta\left(x, y, t\right) &= \epsilon \eta'\left(x, y, t\right), \end{align} where we again let: \begin{align} u' &= - \frac{f_0}{g} \partial_y \eta, \\ v' &= \frac{f_0}{g} \partial_x \eta. \end{align} Plugging these new perturbation quantities into the equation of interest, we get the following: \begin{align} \frac{\textrm{D}}{\textrm{D}t}\left[\zeta + \beta y - f_0 \frac{\eta}{H}\right] = \partial_t\left[\epsilon\partial_xv' - \epsilon\partial_yu' - \epsilon\frac{f_0}{H}\eta' \right] + \overline{u}\left(\epsilon\partial_{x x}v' - \epsilon \partial_{xy}u' - \epsilon \frac{f_0}{H}\partial_x\eta'\right) + \beta v'\partial_yy = 0, \end{align} where we have again neglected all terms higher than $\epsilon$, which leads to the new linearized equation: \begin{equation} \partial_{tx}v' - \partial_{ty}u' + \beta v' - \frac{f_0}{H} \partial_t \eta' + \overline{u}\partial_{x x}v' - \overline{u}\partial_{xy}u' - \overline{u} \frac{f_0}{H}\partial_x\eta' = 0. \end{equation} Plugging in our geostrophic equations for $u', v'$, we get: \begin{equation} \frac{g}{f_0} \partial_{tx x}\eta + \frac{g}{f_0}\partial_{t y y} \eta' + \frac{g}{f_0}\beta\partial_x\eta' - \frac{f_0}{H}\partial_t \eta' + \overline{u}\partial_{x x x}\eta' + \frac{g}{f_0} \overline{u} \partial_{x yy} \eta' - \overline{u} \frac{f_0}\partial_x\eta' = 0. \end{equation} Noting that this is an equation only of sea surface anamoly $\eta'$, we assume a solution of the form $\eta' \sim \eta_0 \exp\left[i\left(kx+ly-\omega t\right)\right]$. Plugging this into our linear equation of $\eta'$ and solving for $\omega$ we get the dispersion relation: \begin{equation} \omega = \frac{k\left(\overline{u}K^2 - \beta\right)}{K^2 + k_d^2} = \overline{u} k - k \frac{\beta + \overline{u}k_d^2}{K^2 + k_d^2}, \end{equation} We note that this is the dispersion relation for Rossby waves with a finite deformation radius linearized around a constant, mean zonal state (i.e., $\underline{u}\not=0.$)

(b) The corresponding components of the phase speed and group velocity are: \begin{equation} c_p^x = \frac{\omega}{k} = \overline{u} - \frac{\beta + \overline{u}k_d^2}{K^2 + k_d^2} \quad, \quad c_p^y = \overline{u}\frac{k}{l} - \frac{k}{l}\left(\frac{\overline{u}K^2 - \beta}{K^2 + k_d^2} \right), \end{equation} and \begin{equation} c_g^x = \overline{u} - \frac{\left(\beta + \overline{u}k_d^2\right)\left(k^2 - l^2 - k_d^2\right)}{\left(K^2 + k_d^2\right)^2} \quad , \quad c_g^y = \frac{2kl\left(\beta + \overline{u}k_d^2\right)}{\left(K^2 + k_d^2\right)^2} \end{equation}

(c) From these equations, we can see that the uniform velocity field provides more than a simple Doppler shift of frequency or just an addition to the phase speed. We can see from $c_p^x$ that the waves are stationary for $K^2 = \beta / \overline{u}$. This is to say that the current speed required to hold waves of a particular wavenumber stationary is $\overline{u} = \beta / K^2$. We note that this is more than just the magnitude of the phase speed of waves of that wavenumber in the absence of a current. Instead, as the current does not provide a uniformation translation but instead modified the basic PV gradient. Specifically, we can see that the $\eta_0$ field is sloping: $\eta' = -(f_0 / g) \overline{u} y$ which means that the ambient PV increases with $y$ and $q = \left(\beta + Uk_d^2\right)y$.

Problem 4

(a) We note that our equation used to solve for the dispersion relation is no longer homogeneous. Instead, we have that the RHS is given by: \begin{equation} -\frac{g}{f_0}\lambda\left(\partial_{x x}\eta' + \partial_{yy}\eta'\right), \end{equation} which gives for our standard $\eta'$ anstaz: \begin{equation} \lambda K^2. \end{equation} This then means that our dispersion is given by: \begin{equation} \omega = \frac{\lambda K^2 + k\left(\overline{u}K^2 - \beta\right)}{K^2 + k_d^2}, \end{equation}

(b) We can see that we have a offset of the form: \begin{equation} \lambda\frac{K^2}{K^2 + k_d^2}. \end{equation} Therefore, we can see that the damping will act proportional to the ratio of the horizontal wave number to the relative contributions of the wavenumber and the Deformation Radius. This then means that we will see a damping will modify the waves along the horizontal wave number propogation, "pushing" the waves further than previously and therefore damping the PV faster.

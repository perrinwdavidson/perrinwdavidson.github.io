Deriving the Equations of Motion for a Liquid on a Rotating Sphere

Newton's Second Law

From any physics course, we know that Newton's Second Law for a parcel $P$ contained within our reference frame $R$ can be stated as: \begin{equation} \mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}, \label{eq:newtons} \end{equation} where we have that $\bm{F}$ is the sum of all forces acting on $P$ with units [M][D][T]$^{-2} \sim $ [N], $m$ is the mass of $P$, and $\bm{a}$ is the acceleration of $P$. \par Now, for $P$ finite-sized (say a box of size $V$ still with mass $m$), then we are good to use this formulation. However, we are interested in discretizing our fluid into volumes of infinitesimal size, $\textrm{d}V$. Defining the density of $P$ to be $\rho$, we note here that $\rho V = m$, so we can update \eqref{eq:newtons} to be, taking $P$ now to be infinitesimal (and thus $V \to \textrm{d}V \to 0$): \begin{equation} \bm{F} = \rho \bm{a}\textrm{d}V. \end{equation} We can redefine our sum of forces as $\bm{\tilde{F}}=\bm{F}/\textrm{d}V \sim $ [N][D]$^{-3}$ and therefore get the updated Newton's Second Law for a Fluid Parcel as: \begin{equation} \bm{\tilde{F}} = \rho \bm{a}. \end{equation} Next, we need to think about all of the forces that are acting on $P$. The ones that we will consider here are: the pressure gradient force, the viscous force, and the gravitational force: \begin{equation} \bm{\tilde{F}} = \bm{\tilde{F}}_p + \bm{\tilde{F}}_v + \bm{\tilde{F}}_g = \rho \bm{a}. \end{equation} I will briefly go through these forces now: \begin{itemize} \item \textbf{Pressure Gradient:} As $P$ is ``pushed'' from higher to lower pressures $(p\sim$ [N][D]$^{-2})$, we can write this as: \[ \bm{\tilde{F}}_p = - \nabla p .\] \item \textbf{Viscous:} We can represent a fluid's resistance to deformation by something like a momentum diffusion process (similar to Fick's Second Law applied to momentum instead of concentration): \[ \bm{\tilde{F}}_v = \mu \nabla ^2 \bm{u} ,\] where $\mu\sim$ [N][T][D]$^{-2}$ is dynamic viscosity. \item \textbf{Gravitational:} The force of a planetary body's gravity pulling $P$ toward it's centre is given by: \[ \bm{\tilde{F}}_g = -\rho g\bm{\hat{k}} = -\rho\nabla \Phi_g, \] where we define $\Phi_g \sim $ [D]$^2$[T]$^{-2}$ to be the gravitational potential of the body. \end{itemize} Now, we note that acceleration of $P$ can be given by the material derivative: \begin{equation} \bm{a} = \frac{\textrm{D}\bm{u}}{\textrm{D}t} = \frac{\partial \bm{u}}{\partial t} + \bm{u} \cdot \nabla\bm{u}. \end{equation} Next, we need to deal with our reference frame $R$, specifically that we are in a non-inertial reference frame (i.e., our fluid is on a sphere, spinning). Therefore, we need to develop some theory to help in accurately describing motion in this reference frame. To do this, let's define vectors $\in \R^2: \bm{f}=[f_1,f_2, f_3]^T$ and $\bm{g}=[g_1, g_2,g_3]^T$ spanned by $[\bm{\hat{\imath}},\bm{\hat{\jmath}},\bm{\hat{k}}]^T$. We now will relate each through a rotation of $\theta$, assuming that one is in an inertial reference frame and one a non-interial, rotating reference frame: \begin{equation} \bm{f}= \begin{bmatrix} \sin \theta & \cos \theta & 0 \\ \cos \theta & -\sin\theta & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \end{equation} where we here related those vectors specifically spanned by $\bm{\hat{\imath}}$ and $\bm{\hat{\jmath}}$.
